Research Program

I am an evolutionary biologist and geneticist studying how genetic variation shapes phenotypic adaptation. My research integrates population genomics, quantitative genetics, and comparative methods with experimental approaches to understand the mechanisms of rapid evolutionary change.

I am currently a Postdoctoral Researcher in the Bolnick Lab at the University of Connecticut. My research uses two complementary approaches to study adaptation:

  • Investigating rapid contemporary adaptation using time-series genomic data from natural host-parasite systems to understand the fitness consequences of immigration and adaptive introgression, as shown in our recent preprint on rapid genome-wide introgression.
  • Employing comparative transcriptomics across diverse fish species to uncover macroevolutionary patterns in gene expression responses to immune stimuli.

I earned my Ph.D. in Biological Sciences at the University of Southern California under Dr. Suzanne Edmands, where I developed the copepod Tigriopus californicus as a model system to study the mitonuclear genetic basis of sex differences in aging and stress tolerance. My doctoral work combined experimental approaches with forward-in-time genetic simulations to unravel how interactions between the nuclear and mitochondrial genomes influence complex life-history traits.

My research program is built on three core pillars:

  • The Genomic Architecture of Rapid Adaptation, using natural and experimental populations to understand the signatures of selection, introgression, and the role of genetic redundancy in trait evolution.
  • Mito-nuclear Interactions and Life-History Evolution, investigating how within-population mitochondrial variation contributes to sex-specific variation in fitness, longevity, and aging.
  • Evolutionary Genomics of Invasive Species, employing species like the red alga Gracilaria vermiculophylla as natural experiments to study the genomic consequences of colonization and establishment.

My work is supported by a combination of experimental and computational approaches, and I am passionate about mentoring the next generation of scientists in quantitative biology and genomics.

I hold an M.S. in Marine Biology from the College of Charleston, where I worked with Dr. Erik Sotka on the population genomics of marine invasions, and a B.S. in Genetics from Clemson University.
